# liveable.melbourne Strategic Plan — Product, Moat, Marketing, and Implementation Backlog

## 1. Strategic thesis

liveable.melbourne should not try to become another property portal. The defensible position is to become the independent property decision layer that buyers and renters use after they find a listing and before they inspect, apply, bid, or offer.

Core positioning:

> The listing tells you why to love it. liveable.melbourne shows what to verify before you commit.

Strategic category:

> Independent property due diligence for buyers and renters.

Primary wedge:

> Paste or enter any Melbourne property address and receive a sourced, plain-English second opinion on liveability, trade-offs, risks, confidence, and verification actions.

## 2. What not to rely on as the moat

Do not treat these as defensible by themselves:

- A liveability score.
- A suburb profile.
- A map UI.
- Aggregating open datasets.
- A generic AI report.
- A list of nearby amenities.

These are useful product ingredients, but Domain, REA, Rent.com.au, View, CoStar, CoreLogic-style firms, and other proptech companies could copy them.

## 3. Real moat

The moat should be a compound moat made of trust, personal context, workflow, user history, and proprietary derived intelligence.

### 3.1 Trust moat

Build a brand around being independent of agents, portals, developers, lenders, insurers, and sponsored listings.

Requirements:

- No agent-paid rankings.
- No lead selling.
- No sponsored suburb or property scores.
- No hidden affiliate ranking logic.
- Every claim has source, date, confidence, geography, and caveat.
- Always state that the product is information only, not legal, financial, insurance, planning, or property advice.

### 3.2 Personal decision moat

A generic report is easy to copy. A report that adapts to a user's life is harder to copy.

Personal context to collect:

- Buy or rent.
- Household type.
- Car ownership.
- Commute destinations.
- Commute days and peak times.
- School zone importance.
- Quiet vs nightlife preference.
- Walkability preference.
- Safety concern level.
- Flood/fire/planning risk tolerance.
- Budget sensitivity.
- Deal-breakers.

The report should answer:

> Is this property a good fit for this person's life, not just whether the suburb has a good score?

### 3.3 Workflow moat

Turn the report into an action system, not just a static page.

Core workflow:

1. User pastes listing/address or drops a pin.
2. User sets personal needs.
3. Product generates property decision report.
4. Product highlights deal-killer risks to verify.
5. User saves property to shortlist.
6. User compares multiple properties.
7. User tracks verification status.
8. User shares/export report with partner, conveyancer, buyer's agent, or family.

### 3.4 Decision-memory moat

Store user-specific decision history:

- Saved properties.
- Rejected properties.
- Reasons for rejection.
- Verification items completed.
- Notes from inspection.
- Shortlist ranking.
- Preferred trade-offs.
- Recurring risk patterns.

This makes liveable.melbourne harder to replace because the product learns the user's decision criteria over time.

### 3.5 Proprietary evidence moat

Open data is copyable. User-verified corrections and inspection evidence are much less copyable.

Build a moderated evidence layer:

- Amenity closed/opened.
- Street feels unsafe at specific times.
- Street flooding observed after heavy rain.
- Construction noise.
- Missing pedestrian crossing.
- Parking pressure.
- Actual walking route issue.
- Planning notice observed.

Rules:

- No gossip.
- No claims about residents.
- No defamatory content.
- Require structured categories and optional evidence/source.
- Keep user submissions separate from official scored data unless verified.

### 3.6 Derived intelligence moat

Do not only expose raw open data. Compute useful derived products:

- Personal fit score.
- Deal-killer risk summary.
- Verification checklist.
- Street-network walking access.
- Commute reliability profile.
- Parcel-level planning/hazard match.
- School zone verification flow.
- Nearby development/supply watch.
- Light/aspect notes.
- Multi-property comparison ranking.

## 4. Product strategy

### 4.1 Product promise

For buyers:

> Before you make an offer, check the risks, trade-offs, and verification steps around the property.

For renters:

> Before you apply or sign, check whether the location actually works for your commute, lifestyle, safety concerns, and daily needs.

For professionals:

> Generate sourced location due-diligence reports for clients, with transparent methodology and verification checklists.

### 4.2 Core user journeys

#### Journey A — First-home buyer

1. Finds property on Domain or realestate.com.au.
2. Pastes address/link into liveable.melbourne.
3. Adds commute, household, school, and risk preferences.
4. Reads top risks and positives.
5. Saves report.
6. Shares with partner/conveyancer.
7. Compares against next property.

#### Journey B — Renter

1. Finds rental listing.
2. Checks location before inspection/application.
3. Prioritises commute, transport, noise, walkability, groceries, and safety.
4. Gets quick decision summary: inspect, maybe, or avoid/verify.
5. Saves shortlist.

#### Journey C — Buyer's agent/conveyancer

1. Uses report credits.
2. Checks multiple properties for clients.
3. Exports/share PDF with methodology appendix.
4. Adds professional notes.

Suggested fields:

- schoolImportance: none | maybe_future | important_now
- quietPreference: low | medium | high
- walkabilityImportance: low | medium | high
- transportImportance: low | medium | high
- safetyConcern: low | medium | high
- riskTolerance: low | medium | high
- budgetSensitivity: low | medium | high

Implementation notes:

- Do not require accounts in MVP.

Acceptance criteria:

- Profile can be edited from report page.
- Report changes visible based on profile.
- User can clear profile data.

### EPIC 4 — Verification checklist

Objective: turn findings into actions.

Checklist item schema:

- id
- propertyId
- category
- title
- whyItMatters
- verifyWith
- sourceUrl/sourceName
- status: not_started | in_progress | checked | not_applicable
- userNotes
- confidence
- geography
- createdAt
- updatedAt

Default checklist categories:

- Flood/fire/planning overlays.
- School zones.
- Insurance quote.
- Council planning certificate.
- Conveyancer review.
- Peak-hour commute.
- Walk the street at night/weekend.
- Nearby development/planning applications.
- Body corporate/owners corporation if apartment.
- Noise/traffic/rail/airport check.

Acceptance criteria:

- User can mark items as checked.
- Status persists locally.
- Checklist appears in print/PDF.
- Report never says “safe” or “approved”; it says “verify.”

Acceptance criteria:

- User can save at least 5 properties.

- Comparison highlights trade-offs, not just winner/loser.

### EPIC 7 — Feedback and correction layer

Objective: begin building proprietary local evidence without compromising trust.

Tasks:

- Add “Report a data issue” form.
- Add categories: amenity closed, amenity missing, wrong location, route issue, observed flooding, construction/planning notice, safety concern, other.

Acceptance criteria:

- Users can submit corrections.
- Site clearly says submissions are reviewed.
- User submissions do not become scored facts automatically.

## 11. Feature prioritisation rule

Prioritise a feature only if it strengthens at least one moat:

1. Trust.
2. Personalisation.
3. Workflow lock-in.
4. Proprietary evidence.
5. Derived intelligence.
6. Distribution.
7. Revenue validation.

If a feature is only “another map layer” and does not improve decision-making, move it below the above roadmap.
